In the year 2000 the Clay Mathematics Institute made a list of seven of the most important open problems in mathematics, which are known as the Millenium Problems. The Clay Institute will award a million dollars for a solution to each of these problems. So far only one problem - the Poincaré conjecture - has been solved. Two of the problems on the list concern number theory, the Riemann Hypothesis and the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture. Both have deep connections to other parts of number theory and mathematics as a whole. I will explain my personal favourite of the two, the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture, often abbreviated as BSD. To understand what BSD says and why it’s important, it’s best to take a walk through history, starting in the Roman Egypt.

This text is a reworked version of a university assignment that I poured too many hours into. The research was very interesting and I stumbled across a seemingly forgotten but inspiring story of how Peter Swinnerton-Dyer did the best work of his career because of a unfortunate setback. So it would be shame not to share it. The intended audience is starting math students and scientists in others fields. So to get all the mathematica details some knowledge is required. However I do hope that there is enough history throughout the text to keep a general audience interested.

The main sources of this document are the talk by Manjul Bhargava [1] and the problem description by the Clay Mathematics Institute [2] for the mathematical details, chapter 14 of Visions of Infinity by Ian Steward [3] and the biography of Diophantus [4] for historical facts. When another source is used it, it will be indicated in the text.

The beginnings of algebra

Diophantus of Alexandria is a mathematician who lived in Alexandria, Egypt in the Roman empire, probably around the third century. He is famous for his series of books Arithmetica in which he presents and solves many mathematical problems. This book is the first known case of symbols being used to represent unknown variables in an equation, similar to modern algebra. His work is distinct from the Greek and Babylonian mathematical schools at the time, because Diophantus dealt neither with geometry nor approximations. Because of this, he sometimes is called the father of algebra. It is a bit ironic that his work developed later into a branch of geometry, called algebraic geometry, which is at the heart of the BSD conjecture.

Arithmetica is somewhat famous among mathematicians because it inspired many problems in number theory. Particularly Pierre de Fermat wrote his famous last theorem in the margin of his copy of Arithmetica, next to problem II,8. A proof of Fermat’s last theorem was discovered more than 350 years later by Andrew Wiles.

In Arithmetica, Diophantus gathered and solved problems like:

II,8

Write \(16\) as the sum of two squares. [5 p. 5]

IV,12

Find numbers \(x\) such that for some \(n\) we have that \(x^3 + n x^2\) is a cube. [6 p. 12]

In modern language most of Diophantus’ problems can be phrased as the same type of problem: Can we find two numbers \(x, y\) that satisfy some polynomial relation

\[f(x, y) = 0.\]

For problem II,8 the polynomail would be \(f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 - 16\) and for IV,12 we need \(f(x, y)= x^3 + n x^3 - y^3\).

Diophantus wanted exact solutions, and he looked for them in the only kind of numbers known at the time, (positive) integers and rational numbers.[1] Polynomial equations where we look for rational solutions are nowadays known as Diophantine equations. Diophantus only knew a collection of tricks, each only applicable in very specific situations. All his solutions were adhoc and a solution to one problem did not translate to a solution for another. While many mathematicians kept on developing Diophantus’ methods, general methods and theorems seemed out of reach. This changed during the 20th century when mathematicians started to discover the deep connections between algebra and geometry and developed the theory now known as Algebraic Geometry.

Turning algebra into geometry

To turn the problem into geometry we need to think about \(x\) and \(y\) as coordinates in a plane. So we rephrase the problem: given a polynomial \(f\) in two variables with integer coefficients, is the set of rational points

\[\mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{Q}}) = \{(x, y) \in {\mathbb{Q}}^2 \mid f(x, y) = 0\}\]

non-empty? If so, how big is it? Can we say anything about its shape?

The notation \(\mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{Q}})\) suggest that we may fill in other kinds of numbers. Indeed, we write \(\mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{R}}), \mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{C}})\) for the sets real and complex points respectively. The letter \(\mathcal{C} \) stands for curve, because if we highlight the real solutions of \(f\) in the \(xy\)-plane, it looks like a curve.

Figure 1. Curves relating to the two diophantine problems above
Figure 2. Louis J. Mordell [7]

In the beginning of the 20th century Louis J. Mordell worked on Diophantine equations and he knew of many examples. He noticed an odd connection. The shape of the complex solutions \(\mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{C}})\) reveals a lot about the number of rational points \(\mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{Q}})\)! In the figure above we saw that \(\mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{R}})\) looks like a curve. Allowing complex solutions gives one more degree of freedom, so \(\mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{C}})\) is a surface, just like the real line becomes a plane when looking at complex numbers. In fact \(\mathcal{C} ({\mathbb{C}})\) looks like a donut, but with maybe more holes cut out (see figure 3). [2] The number of these holes is called the genus of the curve \(\mathcal{C} \) and this is usually denoted by \(g\).

Figure 3. The genus of a complex curve

Mordell’s genius idea was to relate this number of holes to the amount of solutions of a diophantine equation. If \(g = 0\) the curve has either zero of infinite rational points and the case can be determined by an algorithm.

Figure 4. Gerd Faltings [8]

Genus zero curves can always be rewritten to be given by a quadratic polynomial. Quadratic polynomials in two variables satisfy the Hasse Principle, which roughly states that \(f\) has a rational solution if and only if it has a solution modulo every power of every prime. There are algorithms to compute whether this is the case.

If \(g \ge 2\) then Mordell noticed that all the curves he knew of either had no or only finitely many rational points. However he was not able to prove this himself. It was finally proven in 1983 by Gerd Faltings, and is since known as Faltings theorem.

Elliptic curves

The Hasse principle and Faltings theorem give answers to the question of how many rational points there are on curves of genus \(g = 0\) and \(g \ge 2\). That leaves the case of genus \(1\) curves, i.e. curves whose complex points look like a donut. They are called elliptic curves and they pop up all over mathematics, in number theory, physics, cryptography and more. Contrary to what the name suggests, elliptic curves do not look like ellipses! We usually denote elliptic curves by \(\mathcal{E} \) instead of \(\mathcal{C} \). The problem of rational points on these curves is more more difficult because they can have zero, finite or infinite rational points. There are still many open questions relating their rational points.

But Elliptic curves have one big advantage that sets them apart from any other kind of curve. They are secretly a group, or rather their sets of points \(\mathcal{E}({\mathbb{Q}}) , \mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{R}})\) and \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{C}})\) are groups. This means that there is a good notion of adding two real/complex/rational points to get a third point. So besides tools from algebraic geometry, we also have tools from group theory at our disposal to study the rational points.

It is a non-obvious fact that any elliptic curve can be written as

\[f(x,y) = y^2 - (x^3 + ax + b) = 0\]

for some fixed numbers \(a, b\) with \(4a^3 + 27b^2 \ne 0\). The real points of an elliptic curve look something like this:

Figure 5. The Elliptic Curve \(y^2 = x^3 - x + 1\)

This actually does not give the full picture. We need to complete the curve, i.e. add an extra point. As you can see the curve asymptotically goes up and down. You can imagine these two ends meeting up again at some point infinitely far up, or down. We will call this point \(O\).

Addition on an elliptic curve

Let \(A, B\) be the points on \(\mathcal{E} \) we would like to add. Then the sum \(A + B\) can be obtained via the following procedure:

  1. Draw the line \(\ell\) connecting \(A, B\).

  2. Because, the curve is cubic, \(\ell\) intersects \(\mathcal{E} \) in a third point, \(C\).

  3. Mirror \(C\) across the \(x\)-axis to obtain \(A + B = C'\).

Figure 6. Addition on an elliptic curve

We will write \(A'\) for the mirror image of \(A\) along the \(x\)-axis

If \(A, B\), are mirror images, i.e.\ \(B = A'\), then \(\ell\) goes straight up, and we say the third point of intersection is \(O\).

The point \(O\) is special. The line through \(A\) and \(O\) intersects \(\mathcal{E} \) in the mirror image \(A'\). If we mirror back we get \(A'' = A\) again. So \(A + O = A\) and \(O\) acts a zero. This addition is commutative and associative and has identity \(O\).

In the picture we have implicitly assumed that the points are real points. But the same construction works equally well for \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{C}})\) and \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})\).

Understanding the group structure

While Mordell was unable to completely describe the group structure of \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})\) he did prove that \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})\) is a finitely generated group. That means that there are a finite number of points \(P_1, \ldots, P_n\) such that every other rational point can be obtained by adding and subtracting these points.

There are two types of points in \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})\). There are points where repeatedly adding the same point to itself eventually yields the identity \(O\). These points are called torsion points. A fun exercise is to check that any two torsion points add to another torsion point.

On the other hand there are points where repeated addition will keep generating new points. In particular, if such a point exists then \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})\) has infinitely many points.

So the group \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})\) falls apart into two parts, the torsion part and the free part

\[\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}}) = \mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})_\text{tors} \oplus \mathcal{E} ( {\mathbb{Q}})_\text{free}\]

Mordell’s theorem implies that the torsion part is finite and that the free part is isomorphic to \({\mathbb{Z}}^{r}\), the tuples of \(r\) integers, for some \(r\). Nowadays the torsion part is well understood, but the free part has proven to be more difficult to grasp.

The value \(r\) is a way to measure how big the group \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})\) is. If \(r= 0\), then \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{Q}})\) only has torsion points, and thus is finite. On the other hand if \(r \ge 1\), then there is a point that can be repeatedly added to itself to generate an infinite amount of points. This number \(r\) associated to \(\mathcal{E}\) is called the rank of \(\mathcal{E} \) and mathematicians would love to understand the rank better.

The Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture concerns the rank of an elliptic curve.

Computers

Peter Swinnerton-Dyer was a research fellow in mathematics at Trinity College in Cambridge. Unfortunately he was rejected twice for a assistant-lectureship at the maths department. So he took a job at the Cambridge computer lab where he spend ten years. Here he had access to the labs’ fancy new toy, the EDSAC II computer.

Had he not been rejected and forced to take the position in the computer lab, he would have never been able to come up with the conjecture [9]. research position for mathematics in Trinity College.

Had I not been there I don’t think the Birch Swinnerton-Dyer conjectures would ever have happened because they couldn’t have been made credible without the use of a computer
— Peter Swinnerton-Dyer
Interview in Cambridge

At Trinity he met Bryan Birch, another mathematics research fellow. They teamed up to use the EDSAC II computer to compute a lot of explicit examples on elliptic curves. Doing a lot of computations and looking for patterns is certainly not a new idea in mathematics, especially number theory.[3] But the use of a computer for this was new at the time. Birch’s supervisor, J. W. S. Cassels was very sceptical about their approach at first.

Figure 7. Peter Swinnerton-Dyer (left) and Bryan Birch (right) [10]
The EDSAC I and II Computers

EDSAC is an acronym for Electronic Delayed Storage Automatic Calculator. The name refers to the computer’s unique memory system, which uses sound waves to delay information so it could be captured on the next cycle. It was constructed at Cambridge University and ran its first successful programme in 1949. It is considered the first practical computer that stored its programme in memory, instead of needing to be physically rewired.

Figure 8. Replica of the original EDSAC [11]

In 1958 it was replaced by EDSAC II. While it shares the name with its predecessor, it used much faster magnetic memory and was the first computer to be microprogrammable. It also had a much improved arithmetic unit. If you’re interested to learn more about the technical aspects of the EDSAC II you can find more information in [12]

What did Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer exactly compute? So far we have been plugging in real, complex and rational numbers into the equation for elliptic curves. But one can also fill in numbers from \({\mathbb{F}}_p\), the integers modulo some prime number \(p\). Correspondingly we can look at \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{F}}_p)\) the set of all solutions to the equation \(y^2 = x^3 + ax + b\) modulo \(p\). This set and its size is easy to compute. A quick and dirty, but effective way is to simply try each of the \(p^2\) possibilities for \((x, y)\). Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer used a more efficient method.

Figure 9. The \({\mathbb{F}}_p\) points of \(y^2 = x^3 - 4x + 1\)

It is no coincidence that in the curve above there are \(11\) points modulo \(11\). For a given prime \(p\) we actually expect there to be approximately \(p\) points in \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{F}}_p)\), but not exactly. Let \(N_p\) be the number of points in \(\mathcal{E} ({\mathbb{F}}_p)\). Inspired by the Hasse principle and earlier work by Siegel for quadratic equations, they hoped that the values \(N_p\) would reveal something about the rational points on \(\mathcal{E}\).

Like Siegel, they took all primes below some number \(x\) and they looked at the function [13 p. 11]

\[f(x) = \prod_{p \text{ prime} < x} \frac{N_p}{p}.\]

Plotting \(f(x)\) with a logarithmically scaled \(x\)-axis revealed a remarkable pattern. For curves of rank \(0\) they found that \(f(x)\) stayed roughly constant. For a rank \(1\) curve it was a straight but increasing line and for a rank \(2\) curve the graph approximates a parabola!

Figure 10. The data for a rank 0 curve
Figure 11. The data for a rank 1 curve
Figure 12. The data for a rank 2 curve

Their data suggests the following approximation

\[f(x) = \prod _{p \text{ prime} < x} \frac{N_p}{p} \sim C\cdot \log(x)^{r}\]

where \(r\) is the rank of \(\mathcal{E} \) and \(C\) is some constant depending on \(\mathcal{E} \). So the rank is hidden in the numbers \(N_p\)! This is the BSD conjecture, or rather this is one way to state the conjecture.

Links with analysis

Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer went one step further and linked their conclusion to complex analysis, in particular the \(L\)-function of an elliptic curve. \(L\)-functions are cousins of the famous Riemann-Zeta function, but instead of encoding information on prime numbers, the \(L\)-function of an elliptic curve encodes information about the numbers \(N_p\). For elliptic curves the \(L\)-function can be defined as

\[L_{\mathcal{E} }(s) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}}\frac{1}{1 + (N_p - p -1)p^{-s} + p ^{1 - 2s}}.\]

They conjectured that \(L_\mathcal{E} \) has a zero of order \(r\) at \(s = 1\). This roughly means that close to \(s = 1\) the following approximation holds

\[L_{\mathcal{E} }(s) \sim (s - 1)^{r}\cdot c\]

for some constant \(c\). This is the modern phrasing of the conjecture.

This was a bold conjecture, because at the time it was not even known whether \(L\)-functions are well defined for elliptic curves!

The current state

A lot of progress has already been made, yet a lot remains to be done. We know that \(L\)-functions for elliptic curves are well defined. It is also known that if \(L_{\mathcal{E} }\) has a zero of order \(0\) or \(1\) at \(s = 1\) that the rank of \(\mathcal{E} \) is \(0\) or \(1\) respectively. The converse is also true under some extra, but mild assumptions.

However the techniques used to solve the rank \(0\) and \(1\) cases do not generalize to elliptic curves of higher rank. For these curves we still know very little. Probably new ideas are necessary to work on curves of rank \(2\) and higher.

The BSD conjecture has also led to various generalisations and related conjectures, which would answer many questions about rational points beyond curves, like surfaces. So techniques that tackle the BSD conjecture would likely be useful in a much broader context.

It seems like BSD will remain open for a while. But who knows, maybe one day you will find the missing pieces and walk home with a million dollars.
